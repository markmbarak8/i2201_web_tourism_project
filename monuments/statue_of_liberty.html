The Statue of Liberty

explore more about this amazing statue of freedom

Standing tall on Liberty Island in the heart of New York Harbor, the Statue of Liberty—officially titled Liberty Enlightening the World—is perhaps the most iconic symbol of freedom and democracy on the planet. A monumental gift from the people of France to the United States, this UNESCO World Heritage site has served as a welcoming beacon for millions of immigrants arriving by sea for over 135 years. Designed by sculptor Frédéric Auguste Bartholdi with an innovative internal skeleton by Gustave Eiffel, the copper-clad colossus is a masterpiece of 19th-century engineering. From the seven spikes of her crown representing the world's continents to the broken shackles at her feet, every detail of Lady Liberty tells a story of liberty, alliance, and the enduring American dream.

The Statue of Liberty is a triumph of neoclassical design and industrial-age engineering. To support the massive copper exterior, Gustave Eiffel designed a revolutionary central iron pylon and a flexible skeletal framework. This system allows the "skin" of the statue to move independently, meaning Lady Liberty can sway up to 3 inches in high winds (and her torch up to 5 inches) without the metal cracking. This pioneering use of a curtain-wall construction prefigured the engineering used in modern skyscrapers.

Every element of Frédéric Auguste Bartholdi’s sculpture was meticulously chosen to convey a message of universal enlightenment. The robed figure represents Libertas, the Roman goddess of freedom, while the seven rays of her crown symbolize the seven suns, seven seas, and seven continents. Most importantly, the statue was not just a celebration of independence but of the abolition of slavery; Bartholdi placed a broken shackle and chains at her feet—often missed by visitors from a distance—to symbolize the nation moving away from oppression.
